2. Care monitor for traffic

When patient care becomes more concentrated and the number of touchpoints and data inputs grows, care teams need a "single screen" view of the whole patient experience, which John Halamka, executive director of Beth Israel Lahey Health's Health Technology Exploration Center, has cleverly named "care traffic control New instruments and what Halamka has called "a new model for team-based communication and wellness" are needed to orchestrate treatment and incorporate data through an increasingly diverse and potentially virtualized care team. Just as the retail industry has embraced information sharing and omni-channel interaction through CRM platforms, healthcare recognises the need for a coordinated approach to management. In response, several health systems have started to partner with a number of CRM platforms that have built workflows and capabilities to address the specific challenges of patient interaction and enable traffic management of system-wide treatment. For instance, Salesforce partnered with Piedmont Healthcare, a health system serving more than 2 million people across Georgia, to help them deliver, connect and personalise care on a scale. The platform offers the company a common view of the patient, including medical history, insurance, planned appointments, interests, all in one location, putting together data from partner apps and services. By seeing medical background alongside details on jobs, socioeconomic status, and other social determinants of health, Piedmont care teams will get a more comprehensive view of the patient. The same patient profiles can be used by in-home services to flag gaps around items such as safe food accessibility or transportation that could put patients back in the hospital with chronic conditions. Outreach teams may send reminders or run focused campaigns around items like wellness courses, armed with this knowledge and the insights created, to engage patients in more personalised ways. The ultimate aim is to have, at a lower overall cost of treatment, a more organised, personalised patient experience.

3. Navigating the health care data tsunami

Coordinating patient care through an enlarged and more complex world can only become more difficult with the tsunami of information coming from these new outlets, even with the right resources in place. In the next five years, a recent Multinational Data Organization study expects a 36 percent growth rate for healthcare data, higher than in any other sector. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services reacted early in 2019 with a new rule calling on the healthcare industry to take action to allow patients protected, reliable access to their healthcare data and control over it. Finally, we know all too well from recent breaches and scandals how this exposes their data to abuse, as empowering as it is to allow patients control of their data,A new analysis of 24 of the market's top medical apps showed that 79 percent exchanged user information in ways that could compromise privacy. The traditional role of the physician as trusted gatekeeper starts to break down as data passes into patient hands. She has privacy rights if you share health details with your physician. If you share it with Alexa or Google Home, it does not enforce the same privacy rules.
